Statistical Proof

A formal interpretation of the supplied USA heatmap snapshot. The values are Monte Carlo estimates conditional on the simulator’s assumptions and locked results—not guarantees about the actual tournament.

Core conclusion: the heatmap is mathematically internally consistent. It proves what this simulation estimates, not what the future must do.

1. Probability-space definition

Let Ar denote the event that the USA appears in round r, and let Or,t denote the event that its opponent in that round is team t. With N = 10,000 simulated tournaments, the display estimates:

P̂(Ar) = (1/N) Σi=1N 1(Ar(i))
P̂(Or,t) = (1/N) Σi=1N 1(Or,t(i))
P̂(Or,t | Ar) = P̂(Or,t) / P̂(Ar)

Whenever the USA appears in a round, it has exactly one opponent. The matchup events are mutually exclusive and exhaustive:

Ar = ⨆t Or,t   ⇒   Σt P(Or,t) = P(Ar)

This is why every round’s exact matchup chances, including teams outside the top five, sum to its reach chance.

2. What the simulator is modeling

It locks completed results, simulates the remaining group matches, resolves qualification and the knockout bracket, then simulates every knockout match. Team strength is a hand-built composite of baseline/PELE information, PELE trend, pre-tournament title odds, host adjustment, confederation effects, historical World Cup effects, group finish, and round-specific adjustments.

For group matches, the adjusted rating gap creates a logistic home-win share:

πh = logit−1((Sh − Sa) / 180),    d = clip(0.24 − |Sh − Sa| / 2200, 0.05, 0.24)
P(home win) = (1 − d)πh;   P(draw) = d;   P(away win) = (1 − d)(1 − πh)

For knockout matches, the adjusted share is translated to Poisson goal means; scorelines are sampled, with extra time and penalties resolving draws. The knockout win probability is therefore induced by the entire score-generating process rather than being identical to the logistic share.

5. Route, exact chance, and odds

  • Route: P(opponent | USA reaches the round).
  • Exact chance: P(USA reaches the round and faces that opponent).
  • Model-implied American odds: for p < .5, 100(1-p)/p.
Bosnia in R32: p = .354 ⇒ 100(.646/.354) = +182.5 ≈ +182

Those odds are derived from the simulated exact matchup chance. They are not a current sportsbook line.

6. Statistical limits

  • At 10,000 trials, a 30.2% exact chance has roughly ±0.9 percentage points of 95% Monte Carlo sampling error; a 0.9% final matchup has roughly ±0.18 points.
  • Model uncertainty is larger and is not quantified: strength adjustments, historical effects, market weighting, and score-generation choices are assumptions.
  • This is a scenario engine, not a validated causal or Bayesian forecast. A PhD-grade predictive claim would require out-of-sample calibration, parameter estimation, sensitivity analysis, and uncertainty intervals for the model itself.

SO JACK CAN UNDERSTAND

In this simulation, the USA is guaranteed to get into the Round of 32. After that, it gets harder every round.

  • The USA makes the Round of 16 about 3 times out of 4.
  • If it gets there, Egypt is the most likely opponent.
  • If it makes the quarterfinal, Spain is the most likely opponent.
  • If it makes the semifinal, Germany is the most likely opponent.
  • If it makes the final, England is the most likely opponent.

Route means: “If the USA gets this far, who will it probably play?”

Exact chance means: “Out of 100 simulated tournaments, how often does this exact matchup happen?”

Example: USA–Spain in the quarterfinal is 18.3%. The USA gets to the quarterfinal 47.4% of the time, and Spain is on the other side in 38.6% of those cases.
